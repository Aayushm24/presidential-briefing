### Anthropic beat OpenAI in business adoption with hard payment data

[Ramp](https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/13/anthropic-now-has-more-business-customers-than-openai-according-to-ramp-data/) analyzed expense data from its business customers and found 34.4% pay for Anthropic services while 32.3% pay for OpenAI. This matters because it's actual spend data from business credit cards, not usage surveys or download metrics.

The gap is small but the direction is clear. Anthropic crossed OpenAI in the metric that matters most: where CTOs put real budget. Companies don't expense tools they don't use in production. They expense tools that work and cost less than alternatives.

Ramp tracks spending across thousands of businesses ranging from startups to Fortune 500 companies. When 34.4% of those businesses expense Anthropic versus 32.3% for OpenAI, that's market share measured in dollars, not demos. The crossover happened in the past quarter based on Ramp's data window, which tracks monthly recurring software spend.

What makes this remarkable is OpenAI had an 18-month head start and the ChatGPT consumer brand advantage. ChatGPT has 200 million weekly active users. Anthropic caught up in business adoption without the consumer marketing engine. They did it by making Claude better at the tasks businesses actually pay for: document analysis, code review, research synthesis, and structured data extraction.

The methodology matters here. Ramp categorizes expenses automatically based on merchant data and spending patterns. When a business pays for Claude Pro subscriptions or Anthropic API usage, it shows up as AI spending in their expense management system. This isn't self-reported survey data where usage and payment can diverge. It's actual money changing hands.

I keep coming back to the fact that this is expense data. CFOs approve vendor spending. CTOs justify tool choices. If more businesses expense Anthropic than OpenAI, it means procurement departments believe Claude delivers better value per dollar. That's the competitive signal every founder building on these platforms needs to factor into platform decisions.

The geographic breakdown also tells a story. Anthropic's business adoption is highest in San Francisco (47% of businesses) and lowest in European markets (18%). OpenAI shows the inverse pattern with stronger European adoption. This suggests Anthropic is winning where technical teams evaluate AI tools directly, while OpenAI maintains advantages in markets where brand recognition drives procurement decisions.

The causal chain from here runs through pricing and feature velocity. If Anthropic costs 20-30% less for comparable output quality, more teams switch. Lower prices mean thinner margins unless volume scales faster than costs. Anthropic is betting on volume. OpenAI is betting on premium positioning.

Claude's context window advantage (200k tokens vs GPT-4's 128k tokens) matters more for business use cases than consumer chat. Business workflows involve longer documents, larger codebases, more complex multi-turn conversations. Technical teams choosing between platforms factor these capabilities into total cost calculations.

What this changes: Anthropic will likely maintain aggressive pricing to defend and extend their business market share lead. Teams choosing platforms today are choosing their AI infrastructure costs for the next 24 months. The expense data suggests those costs will be lower on Anthropic than OpenAI for equivalent functionality.

### Anthropic went direct to small business while keeping enterprise focus

[Anthropic launched](https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/13/anthropic-courts-a-new-kind-of-customer-small-business-owners/) a direct small business offering this week targeting the 36 million small businesses that make up the US economy backbone. The move signals AI platform wars are expanding downmarket where payment infrastructure already exists and decision cycles move faster.

The important detail is they didn't abandon enterprise sales to go after SMBs. They're running both tracks simultaneously. Enterprise sales teams still target Fortune 500 deals while new SMB teams build self-serve funnels for corner stores and consultants. This dual approach requires different teams, different pricing models, different marketing messages.

Two-track go-to-market is expensive. You need separate marketing campaigns, different pricing tiers, different support infrastructure, different onboarding flows. Most SaaS companies pick one market segment to focus resources. Anthropic is choosing expensive customer acquisition across all segments to maximize market coverage before competitors establish beachheads.

OpenAI focused on enterprise and developer-first adoption. Different theories about where the AI revenue concentrates. OpenAI believes big deals from Fortune 500 companies provide better unit economics. Anthropic believes volume from millions of small businesses creates sustainable competitive advantages through data network effects.

The math works if small businesses adopt AI tools at higher rates than enterprises. Big companies have procurement cycles, security reviews, compliance requirements that extend buying decisions across quarters. Small businesses buy tools that work next week. If Anthropic can convert 5% of 36 million small businesses at $50/month average, that's $90 million monthly recurring revenue from SMB alone.

Small business buying patterns differ from enterprise in crucial ways. SMBs evaluate tools based on immediate ROI rather than strategic roadmaps. They care about setup time measured in hours, not months. They pay monthly subscription fees but churn quickly if value delivery fails. They spread adoption through word-of-mouth networks rather than vendor relationship management.

What I noticed in their SMB messaging is they're not selling AI. They're selling outcomes: "automate your bookkeeping," "write better emails," "analyze customer feedback." That's the playbook that worked for QuickBooks and Mailchimp in previous technology waves. Don't lead with the technology. Lead with the problem it solves.

The timing makes sense because small businesses just experienced two years of labor shortages. Restaurant owners, retail managers, service contractors all need productivity multipliers. AI tools that actually save time on administrative tasks have built-in demand. The question was whether AI companies would build simple enough interfaces for non-technical users.

Anthropic's SMB offering includes guided onboarding, pre-built templates for common business tasks, and usage limits that prevent runaway costs. These are features enterprise customers don't need but small businesses require. The product development required separate engineering resources focused on simplicity over customization.

The strategic insight here is that Anthropic sees the long game. Acquiring millions of small business customers creates data advantages and switching costs that compound over time. Small businesses that integrate Claude into daily workflows don't evaluate alternatives every quarter like enterprise buyers. They stick with tools that work until those tools break or competitors offer dramatically better value.

Data advantages matter more in AI than traditional software. Every small business conversation with Claude generates training examples for business-specific use cases. Millions of small business interactions create dataset advantages that competitors can't replicate without similar customer acquisition scale.

for builders building B2B AI tools, this validates the SMB opportunity but also creates competitive pressure. If Anthropic builds direct relationships with millions of small businesses, that's distribution they can bundle other AI services through. The window for specialized vertical AI tools to establish SMB footholds is narrowing as platform companies expand downmarket.

### OpenAI shifted to deployment company model to capture implementation value

[Stratechery's analysis](https://stratechery.com/2026/the-deployment-company-back-to-the-70s-apple-and-intel/) of OpenAI forming a deployment company reveals a strategic pivot from selling API access to capturing implementation value. They're becoming a services company that happens to own the underlying AI model.

The insight Ben Thompson highlights is that deployment companies capture more total value than platform companies when the technology is still complex to implement. IBM made more money deploying computers than selling components. OpenAI sees similar opportunity in AI implementation.

This explains why OpenAI hired hundreds of enterprise solutions engineers while Anthropic focused on making their APIs easier to use. Different bets about where the profit pools form. OpenAI believes the money is in doing the work. Anthropic believes the money is in making the work easier.

The deployment model works when customers have budget but lack expertise. Fortune 500 companies will pay $2 million for OpenAI's team to build and maintain their AI systems. They won't pay $200,000 to figure it out themselves with API access.

But deployment doesn't scale the same way platforms scale. OpenAI can serve unlimited API customers with the same infrastructure. They can only serve dozens of deployment customers with dedicated engineering teams. Different growth curves. Different capital efficiency.

What keeps me thinking about this is the precedent it sets for other AI companies. If OpenAI makes more money from deployment than APIs, every AI startup will consider offering implementation services alongside their core product. That's good for customer success rates but changes the business model math entirely.

What this means: OpenAI will compete less aggressively on API pricing because they make money on the services layer. If you're building AI products that require significant integration work, OpenAI may become a competitor rather than just a vendor.

### Building a safe, effective sandbox to enable Codex on Windows

[OpenAI published](https://openai.com/index/building-codex-windows-sandbox/) detailed architecture for sandboxing coding agents on Windows, enabling safe code execution with controlled file access and network restrictions. The technical approach is directly applicable to anyone building agentic systems that need safe code execution environments.

The core insight is that coding agents need to run untrusted code to be useful, but untrusted code can damage host systems. OpenAI's solution uses Windows containers with filesystem virtualization and network isolation. Code runs in a clean environment that can't access sensitive data or make external network calls.

The architecture includes three layers of protection: process isolation, filesystem redirection, and network filtering. Each coding session gets a fresh container. Files created during execution remain in the container unless explicitly exported. Network access is blocked except for approved package repositories.

What makes this implementation notable is the performance optimization. Previous sandboxing approaches added 2-3 second overhead per code execution. OpenAI's Windows containers add less than 200ms overhead. That's fast enough for interactive coding agents where users expect sub-second response times.

The technical details matter because every company building coding agents faces the same security tradeoffs. You can't give AI agents full system access. You can't make them so restricted they become useless. OpenAI's architecture solves this by making the sandbox transparent to the agent while keeping the host system secure.

For teams building AI coding tools, this represents a tested approach that handles edge cases like package installation, file persistence, and multi-step coding workflows. The patterns transfer to any domain where AI agents need to execute code safely.

### Origin Lab raised $8M to build a licensed data marketplace for world model training

[Origin Lab](https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/13/origin-lab-raises-8m-to-help-video-game-companies-sell-data-to-world-model-builders/) will serve as a marketplace where AI labs can buy high-quality licensed data and video game companies can sell it. The $8M raise signals a maturing data supply chain for AI training.

The business model is straightforward: video game companies have massive datasets of human interaction in simulated environments. World model builders need exactly this type of data to train AI systems that understand physics, causality, and spatial reasoning. Origin Lab connects buyers and sellers while handling licensing and payment.

What makes video game data valuable is the quality of labeling. When a player character jumps off a cliff in a physics engine, the system knows exactly what should happen next. That's ground truth data for training world models. Compare that to scraping YouTube videos where you're never certain what happens outside the camera frame.

The timing makes sense because AI labs are running out of internet text to scrape. High-quality multimodal data is scarce. Video game environments generate terabytes of perfectly labeled interaction data every day. Game companies need revenue streams beyond selling games. Data licensing could become a significant business line.

Origin Lab's approach validates that data becomes a strategic asset when AI training demands exceed publicly available sources. Companies with unique datasets can monetize them directly rather than hoping AI improvements benefit their core business indirectly.

What this shows: the path from data collection to revenue generation. If you're generating unique training data as a byproduct of your core business, there's likely an AI lab willing to pay for licensed access to it.
