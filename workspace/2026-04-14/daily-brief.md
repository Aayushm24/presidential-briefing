# Daily Brief — 2026-04-14

**Lead:** Not all AI agents are created equal

The Agent Layer Is Splitting in Two. Pick Your Side.

Here's the pattern showing up across multiple stories today: AI agents have crossed the line from "interesting demo" to "thing companies are actually deploying, staffing for, and spending real money on." But as that happens, a clear split is emerging. On one side, platform giants like Microsoft are absorbing generic agent capabilities into their existing products, making basic agentic functionality a commodity feature. On the other side, companies going deep on specific domains, like retail operations, compliance workflows, and enterprise sales, are building agents that actually own outcomes in ways that are hard to replicate.

If you're building in the agent space right now, this bifurcation is the most important strategic question you face. Let me walk through the evidence.

Evidence 1: Lenny's Framework Reveals the Complexity Spectrum

Hamza Farooq and Jaya Rajwani published a detailed framework in Lenny's Newsletter for categorizing agent initiatives. The core insight is deceptively simple: most teams are failing at agent prioritization because they're treating fundamentally different systems as if they're the same thing.

A PM assistant you can wire together in n8n in six weeks is not the same category of problem as a voice-enabled shopping agent that requires dedicated ML engineering, custom infrastructure, and generates six-figure LLM bills. Yet teams keep throwing both onto the same impact-vs-effort matrix and wondering why their planning feels like guesswork.

The framework breaks agents into three tiers based on architectural complexity. The details are behind the paywall, but the directional insight is what matters here: the lower tiers (automation workflows, retrieval-augmented copilots) are increasingly table-stakes capabilities that platforms will absorb. The higher tiers (reasoning systems, multi-agent networks with real operational autonomy) are where defensible value lives, and they require fundamentally different investment.
