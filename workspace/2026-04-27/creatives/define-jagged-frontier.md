# Define — jagged-frontier

## Source post

Everybody these days is an AI automation expert.

It's a great service to sell to founders.

A founder will see AI excel in one domain. Then assume it works the same way everywhere else.

They pitch "80% automation" without understanding the remaining 20% might be the most critical part.

The pattern shows up everywhere:

- Marketing thinks sales is "just sending emails."
- Sales ops thinks accounting is "just entering numbers."
- Product thinks customer service is "just answering questions."

All three are wrong about what AI can replace.

The jagged frontier is real.

AI writes perfect code for complex algorithms but fails at counting characters in a string.

It handles creative tasks that seemed impossible two years ago and breaks on basic reasoning any human solves instantly.

Teams build for the peaks. Crash on the valleys.

When building with AI, the question that matters more than "what can AI do" is "where should it stop."

The successful automation tools I track all have one thing in common.

They don't promise full automation.

They design human handoff points from day one.

AI handles what it's good at. Humans handle the rest. The interface between them IS the product.

That's augmentation, not automation.

The experts selling 80% automation are skipping the part that decides whether their tool ships or fails.

What's the 20% in your stack that AI absolutely cannot touch?

## Position statement

Make a builder feel that the "80% automation" pitch they were considering is naive — and want to redesign their tool around the handoff point before they build the wrong thing.

## 3 conviction bets (specific, falsifiable)

1. **The chart IS the post.** A literal jagged-line capability chart, with two specific tasks labeled (one peak, one valley), will outperform any typographic treatment because the post's central metaphor ("jagged frontier") is already visual. Forcing the viewer's eye to trace peak-to-valley does the interpretive work that copy would otherwise have to do.
2. **One word of accent, not a slogan.** A single brick-red word — "VALLEY" or "20%" — placed where the eye lands first will out-stop any 5-word headline. The post does the talking; the creative is the hook.
3. **Hand-drawn beats vector-clean.** A subtly imperfect line (slight stroke variance, off-grid label placement, monospaced annotations) reads as "a builder sketched this on a napkin" — which matches the post's voice. A polished vector chart reads as McKinsey deck and kills trust.

## Kill list

1. Will NOT use a brain-with-circuit-lines, neural-net swooshes, or any AI-stylized decoration.
2. Will NOT use stock illustrations, gradient backgrounds, or 3D rendered shapes.
3. Will NOT add a headline like "The Jagged Frontier" — the post owns that phrase; the image must not repeat it.
4. Will NOT use multiple accent colors. One brick-red on warm-neutral canvas, full stop.
5. Will NOT center the composition. Top-left anchor with off-axis chart placement.
6. Will NOT use emoji or decorative chart icons.
7. Will NOT exceed 12 words of text on the canvas.
8. Will NOT include Aayush's handle, Atlan logo, or any brand mark — the artifact stands alone.
9. Will NOT use rounded-corner cards or drop shadows beyond the receipt-style hairline.
10. Will NOT explain the metaphor with a subtitle. If it needs a subtitle, the visual failed.

## Success criteria

- **3-second test:** Viewer registers (1) a jagged line/contrast, (2) the peak-vs-valley relationship, (3) one specific labeled task that demonstrates the gap. They do NOT need to read a headline to get the point.
- **Thumbnail test:** At 400px wide, the jagged shape and the single accent word/number are still readable. Labels can blur — the silhouette carries the meaning.
- **Logo-swap test:** If you put a McKinsey logo in the corner, it should feel WRONG — the imperfection, monospaced labels, and off-grid placement signal "builder", not "consultant deck". That's the tell.

## Design constraints

- **Color palette:** ONE accent — brick-red (`#a8301f`, callback to receipt). Canvas warm-neutral (`#f4efe6` paper or `#f8f6f0` off-white). Ink near-black (`#141414`).
- **Typography:** IBM Plex Mono for labels and annotations (matches receipt voice). Inter 800 only if a hero number is used. No mixed sans-serif.
- **Mandatory elements:** Some form of peak-vs-valley contrast. One specific task name from the post (e.g., "complex algorithms" / "counting characters") OR one specific number ("80%" / "20%").
- **Forbidden elements:** see kill list.
- **Dimensions:** 1080×1080 (per task brief — square LinkedIn).
