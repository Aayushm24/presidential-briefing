# Hook Patterns

Proven hook structures. The hook must fit in 80 characters (LinkedIn mobile fold before "see more").

## Pattern 1: Credential + reversal
"The person who built [impressive thing] just stopped doing [expected thing]."
Works when: a credible person does something surprising.
Example: "The person who built Tesla's Autopilot just stopped using AI to write code."

## Pattern 2: Specific number + surprising result
"[Exact number] [things] and [counterintuitive finding]."
Works when: data tells a story that contradicts expectations.
Example: "97M installs and nobody is talking about what it actually means."

## Pattern 3: Two facts that don't fit together
"[Fact A]. [Fact B]. Something doesn't add up."
Works when: two data points create tension.
Example: "Cursor raised $900M. Copilot loses money on every user."

## Pattern 4: Direct claim
"[Bold statement about the industry]."
Works when: you have a conviction backed by evidence.
Example: "The AI coding assistant market peaked this quarter."

## Pattern 5: Named entity + unexpected action
"[Company/person] just [did unexpected thing]."
Works when: a major player makes a surprising move.
Example: "Meta's first model from its new lab is deliberately small."

## Pattern 6: Binary tension
"[Option A] or [Option B]. The answer changes everything."
Works when: there's a real architectural or strategic fork.
Example: "More parameters or smarter architecture. The answer determines who wins."

## Rules for all hooks

- Under 80 characters when possible. Never over 120.
- Specific > generic. Named companies, exact numbers, real people.
- No questions as hooks (engagement bait — penalized by LinkedIn algorithm).
- No "I" as the first word.
- No colons in hooks (they look like templates).
- The hook should create a gap — the reader needs to know what comes next.
