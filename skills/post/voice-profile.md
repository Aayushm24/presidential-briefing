# Voice Profile: Aayush

## Who Aayush is as a creator

Aayush builds AI systems and advises founders on AI strategy. His LinkedIn content shows founders what AI actually looks like in practice — not the hype, not the theory, but the tools, the outcomes, and the decisions that matter. He values authenticity and never fabricates details, stats, or examples.

## Audience

Founders building with AI. CEOs, CTOs, technical co-founders making product decisions. They don't care about PPO, RLHF, or attention mechanisms. They care about:
- what can I build with AI right now?
- what tools should I try this week?
- what's actually working vs what's hype?
- how do I avoid wasting time and money?

Don't write "for founders" explicitly. Don't address them as "hey founders." The posts should NATURALLY appeal to builders by being about: outcomes, tools, real numbers, real stories, real decisions. A founder reads it and thinks "this is exactly what I needed to know" without being told it's for them.

Aayush's positioning: the person who builds with AI, tracks everything happening in the space, and shares what actually works.

Test: Would someone building an AI product save this post and try something from it this week?

## How Aayush writes

Sentence length: Short. One idea per line. Fragments are good.

Vocabulary: Fifth-grade English. Simple words. "Use" not "utilize."

Case: lowercase i. lowercase everything except proper nouns.

Tone: Self-deprecating humor mixed with genuine expertise. Casual — "IMO", "ie", "we'll", "I think", "stupidly simple."

Formatting:
- Dashes (-) NEVER bullets (•)
- Bold sparingly — 3-5 words max per post
- One idea per line
- Heavy line breaks for rhythm
- Hook under 70 characters (one line on mobile)

Numbers: Specific. "$900M" not "nearly a billion." "47 wallets" not "dozens."

Contractions: Always. "doesn't" not "does not."

## What makes the posts Aayush shares work

From analyzing posts he likes and shares:

1. STORIES not concepts. "An ex-OpenAI engineer walked up to me" not "Here's how PPO works."
2. SPECIFIC tools/repos/links. Named. Linked. Founders save these.
3. SPECIFIC numbers. Revenue, cost, team size, time to build. "$25/month. No team. No office."
4. OUTCOMES not process. "16 days. 187 trades. 71% win rate. $800 seed. +$8,700."
5. The reader thinks "i should try this" not "i understand this concept."
6. NO jargon without a plain-english explanation first.
7. The insight is about HOW TO USE AI WELL, not how AI works internally.

## Emotion distribution

40% vulnerability ("i kept waiting for the catch. the catch never came.")
30% practical insight ("three commands. the bot sees 500+ markets in real time.")
20% wisdom ("the hardest engineering skill is knowing which problems to stop solving.")
10% humor/surprise ("he looked at me like I was an idiot.")

## The test

Does this post feel INEVITABLE, not ENGINEERED?
Would someone read this and DO something differently today?
Could you swap Aayush's name for any generic "AI thought leader" and it still works? If yes, it fails.

## What Aayush does NOT sound like

- Academic: no "Furthermore, the implications suggest..."
- Newsletter host: no "Welcome to this week's edition..."
- Generic AI person: no "AI is changing everything..."
- ML researcher: no jargon without plain-english explanation
- Motivational: no "The future belongs to those who..."

## Principles

1. Never fabricate details or examples. Real experiences only.
2. Lead with what the AUDIENCE cares about (outcomes, tools, money), not the technology.
3. Name specific tools and repos. People save these.
4. Use real numbers. Revenue, cost, time, team size.
5. Self-deprecating humor > authority positioning.
6. Permission-giving > prescription. "Take this as your sign to..." beats "You should..."
7. One sharp idea per post. Not a listicle.

## Learned patterns (from edit diffs)

### Pattern 1: No "It's like X" analogies
Turn analogies into direct questions to the reader. Use "you and I" language. ALL CAPS for emphasis. Fragment endings ("Probably not.").

### Pattern 2: Audience-first hooks
Open with something the audience has EXPERIENCED, not the paper title. The technical concept comes AFTER the hook, explained through familiar analogies. The insight is the PRINCIPLE, not the technique.

### Pattern 3: Stories > explanations
"An ex-OpenAI engineer walked up to me" beats "SPPO addresses core PPO limitations." Discovery narratives (someone showed me → I tried it → here's what happened) outperform concept explanations every time.
