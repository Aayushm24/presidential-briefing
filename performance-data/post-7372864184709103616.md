## Content

> I finally broke GPT…
> I asked how many r’s are in “strawberry.”
>
> It said 2.
>
> Meanwhile a five-year-old 
> “laughing” and “counting”
>
> S-T-R-A-W-B-E-R-R-Y. (3.)
>
> This trillion-dollar technology that can write code, pass medical exams, and explain quantum physics... 
>
> Can't count to 3.
>
> Why?
>
> LLMs are “like” your phone’s autocomplete
>
> You type: “I’m heading to the…”
> Your phone: Gym, store, office.
>
> It does’t access your calendar, rather completes a common pattern.
>
> LLMs do the same. 
> So when you ask, “How many r’s in strawberry?” 
> It’s trying to guess or predict a number
>
> Words with three of the same letter are uncommon, so the model goes: “Probably 1… maybe 2.”
>
> Elephant memory, goldfish math.
>
> Meanwhile we ask AI to:
> → Analyze customer data
> → Count sales trends
> → Track inventory
>
> …and it’s out here failing kindergarten.
>
> So what’s the fix?
> → Make it list things first vs direct counting
> → Use code for counting. "strawberry".count("r") → 3
> → Double-check. “Do it twice independently; if different, recount.”
> → Force proof. “Line 1: the number. Line 2: the positions you used.”
>  
> Quick test:
>
> “How many p’s in hippopotamus?”
> Correct: 3.
> ChatGPT (too often): “Definitely 2.”
>
> This is honestly the funniest thing I’ve learned all year.
