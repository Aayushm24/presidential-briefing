## Content

> Every SaaS is shipping an AI button right now. Most of them are going to look ridiculous in 18 months.
>
> - Notion has one.
> - Linear has one.
> - Slack has one.
>
> Every tool in my stack now has a little sparkle icon in the corner.
> Click it, chat box opens, "hi, how can i help?"
>
> The button isn't the problem.
> The user it's designed for is.
>
> Every AI button ships with the same unstated assumption: a human is inside the app, clicking around, and the AI's job is to make that human faster.
>
> But that's the part that's changing.
>
> When we build agents at Atlan, we don't have them click buttons.
> - They call APIs.
> - They read from databases.
> - They talk to other apps through MCP's
> -  They do the whole job and post the result someplace we want
>
> The human never opens the app.
>
> Some headlines from this week to back my hypothesis: 
>
> Figma's bet: designers stay in Figma, AI makes them faster inside it.
>
> Anthropic's bet: designers don't open Figma. They describe what they want to Claude.
>
> Same user. Two very different futures.
>
> OpenAI's Codex update shipped computer use (the agent literally sees your screen and operates your apps for you), an in-app browser, and 90+ plugins, many of them MCP servers.
>
> The head of Codex said it out loud: "we're building the superapp in the open."
>
> IMO, here's what most product teams aren't thinking yet:
>
> - You're not in a race to make your app AI-powered. 
> - You're in a race against the interaction layer itself shifting.
> - The question isn't "did we add AI to our product? 
> - It's "who's going to be using our product 18 months from now.
>
> If the answer is an agent, the sparkle button is aimed at a user who's already walking out the door.
>
> The things that actually matter when your user is an agent:
>
> -  .md files
> - Clean, well-documented APIs and MCP servers
> - Clear approval gates where a human stays in the loop
> - Outputs that drop into the channels humans actually live in
> - The data and logic an agent can't easily replicate somewhere else
>
> None of that is a button.
> None of it ships in a corner of your nav bar.
>
> I'm not saying UIs die.
>
> Humans will still want nice interfaces for the work they still do directly.
>
> But a huge chunk of software was built on the assumption that the human is the operator.
>
> That assumption is breaking (quietly).
>
> The companies that see it early will build for the new user.
>
> The ones that don't will ship a prettier sparkle button next quarter and wonder why engagement is flat.
>
> Add AI to your product, sure.
> But ask the harder question first: who's actually going to be using this thing?
>
> Because they might not be who you think.
